How We Started

The Open Doors Project was inspired by team members’ noticing a growing crisis of identity and direction among their peers, an issue that they observed leading to aimlessness, isolation, and depression—all of these experiences only exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic. We feel that addressing this phenomena and seeking out the root of its cause could lead to targeted improvements in the support students are able to find within the education system.

From script development, to casting for participants, to the filming of interviews and video editing, every aspect of our production has been run and realized by a passionate group of roughly two dozen young adults including undergraduate students, high school seniors, and military personnel from across the United States. While the project is the result of contributions from individuals all over the country, the on-ground filming of our interviews took place in Boston and New York City, each production with localized casting and camera crew teams. In scouting for participants, we strove for geographic (in addition to other aspects of) diversity among our interviewees, meaning that our participants placed their hometowns in a variety of locations throughout the country. Between our two studio locations in Boston and New York, a total of 15 interviews were collected and featured in our initial video.

With the perspective that we, and hopefully other viewers, gain from these interviews, we hope to learn and spread a message of how we might best improve the educational system in our country in its support of students and their sense of purpose.

Who We Are

College students from Stanford, Harvard, BU, Brandeis, Columbia, Lesley, Wellesley, and the Claremont Compound, working with high school students and military service members. We are personally invested in issues surrounding public education, equity, and mental health in the United States. Isabella Pistaferri

Producer. Isabella is a member of the Stanford University class of 2025. Her academic focus lies mainly within the fields of psychology and communications, as well as feminist studies, American liberal studies, and art practice. Her work in education first began at Henry M. Gunn High school in Palo Alto, where she co-founded and served as vice president of Gunn’s Hands Off IX Club. Through Hands Off IX, Isabella focused on raising awareness to issues regarding gender inequality and Title IX in Palo Alto schools. She gained hands-on experience conversing with local school board officials and educators; collaborating with other high school and university students; proposing, campaigning for, and passing local Title IX reform; and curating, then releasing information regarding student rights under Title IX through easily digestible forms of media. At Stanford, Isabella has explored the power of art and media to convey messages of importance through, for example, zine-making for social and political activism.
